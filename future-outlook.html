Section 04 — The ZCash Fallacy

Privacy Theater vs. Privacy by Default

ZCash is often cited as a Monero alternative. A comprehensive investigation of its history, governance, cryptography, and on-chain data reveals a privacy coin that fails at its one job: providing privacy.

4.1 — Origin Story
The Corporate Genesis of ZCash

ZCash did not emerge from a cypherpunk mailing list, an anonymous whitepaper, or a grassroots community. It was born in a boardroom. In 2016, the Electric Coin Company (ECC) — originally called the Zerocoin Electric Coin Company — was founded by Zooko Wilcox-O'Hearn, a computer scientist with deep ties to Silicon Valley venture capital. Before a single block was mined, ZCash had already raised institutional funding from Pantera Capital, Digital Currency Group (Barry Silbert), Naval Ravikant, Roger Ver, Erik Voorhees, and several other prominent crypto investors.

This is not incidental. The funding structure determined everything that followed: the governance model, the economic incentives, the regulatory posture, and ultimately, the decision to make privacy optional. When your investors include some of the most compliance-friendly names in crypto, building a coin that actually threatens the surveillance state was never on the roadmap.

The underlying cryptography — zk-SNARKs (Zero-Knowledge Succinct Non-Interactive Arguments of Knowledge) — originated from academic research by Eli Ben-Sasson, Alessandro Chiesa, Christina Garman, Matthew Green, Ian Miers, Eran Tromer, and Madars Virza. The science was real. The implementation choices were political.

4.2 — Follow the Money
The 20% Dev Tax Problem

From its first block, ZCash imposed a 20% tax on all mining rewards. For every 12.5 ZEC mined, 2.5 ZEC went not to the miner who secured the network, but to the Electric Coin Company, investors, and affiliated entities. This was called the "Founders' Reward" — a euphemism for what is, in practice, a perpetual corporate subsidy paid by the network's own security providers.

When the original Founders' Reward expired in November 2020, the community was presented with a governance vote: end the tax, or continue it. The result was ZIP 1014, which restructured but preserved the 20% allocation. The new "Dev Fund" split proceeds three ways: 8% to ECC, 7% to the Zcash Foundation, and 5% to a Major Grants Fund. The tax was set to continue through the next halving cycle.

Over ZCash's lifetime, the 20% allocation has directed approximately 3.5 million ZEC to the ECC, Foundation, and grant recipients — out of a total ~16 million ZEC mined. That means roughly one in five coins ever created went to insiders, not to the miners who actually secure the chain.

4.3 — Cryptographic Trust Debt
The Trusted Setup: "Trust Us, We Deleted the Keys"

ZCash's original privacy system (zk-SNARKs using the Groth16 proving system) required a trusted setup ceremony. This is a one-time cryptographic event where secret parameters — called "toxic waste" — are generated and then must be destroyed. If anyone retains this toxic waste, they gain the ability to create counterfeit ZEC out of thin air, completely undetectable by the network.

The Sprout ceremony (2016) involved six participants who each contributed randomness via a multi-party computation protocol. The security guarantee: as long as at least one participant honestly destroyed their share of the toxic waste, the system is secure. The participants included Zooko Wilcox himself, along with five others who performed their computations in various locations using various hardware.

The fundamental problem is not whether the participants were honest. The problem is that the claim is unfalsifiable. There is no mathematical proof, no on-chain evidence, and no cryptographic verification that can demonstrate the toxic waste was destroyed. You must simply trust that six humans — including the CEO of the company that profits from ZCash — acted correctly. This is the exact opposite of the "don't trust, verify" ethos that cryptocurrency was built to embody.

The Sapling ceremony (2018) scaled this to 87 participants via the "Powers of Tau" protocol, which improved the trust assumption but did not eliminate it. You still must trust that at least one of those 87 individuals was honest and not compromised. The Orchard upgrade (2022) finally adopted Halo 2, which requires no trusted setup for the new shielded pool — but the old Sprout and Sapling pools, and the coins minted under their trust assumptions, remain part of the protocol forever.

4.4 — The Killer Statistic
Shielded vs. Transparent: ~10% Privacy in a "Privacy Coin"

This is the single most damning fact about ZCash: approximately 90% of all ZCash transactions are fully transparent. They use t-addresses (transparent addresses) that work exactly like Bitcoin — sender, receiver, and amount are all visible on the public blockchain. Only transactions between z-addresses (shielded addresses) enjoy full privacy, and these represent a small minority of on-chain activity.

The reasons for this are structural, not accidental. Exchanges default to transparent addresses because regulators require it. Wallets default to transparent because shielded transactions are computationally heavier and take longer to process. Most ZCash users have never sent a shielded transaction in their lives. The "privacy coin" label is marketing, not reality.

Even worse: transactions that move between transparent and shielded pools (t-to-z or z-to-t) leak metadata. When you shield 10 ZEC from a transparent address, observers know that the t-address owner has 10 ZEC in the shielded pool. When you unshield later, they can correlate amounts and timing. The only fully private flow is z-to-z (shielded-to-shielded), which represents the smallest fraction of all ZCash activity.

Why Optional Privacy Is No Privacy

When privacy is optional, using it becomes a signal. If 90% of transactions are transparent and you choose to shield yours, you have flagged yourself as someone with something to hide. This is the fundamental paradox: optional privacy is worse than no privacy at all, because it creates a two-tier system where the "private" tier is a smaller, more conspicuous anonymity set. In Monero, every transaction looks the same — there is no "suspicious" behavior because privacy is the only option.

Cryptographer Ian Miers — one of ZCash's own co-creators — has acknowledged this dynamic. The academic literature on anonymity sets is clear: privacy systems are only as strong as the number of users who use them identically. A shielded pool used by 10% of participants provides fundamentally weaker privacy guarantees than a system where 100% of transactions are indistinguishable. This is not a matter of opinion. It is information theory.

4.6 — Surveillance Compatibility
Chainalysis and ZCash: Already Traced

Chainalysis, Elliptic, and CipherTrace all support ZCash transparent address tracing. Since approximately 85-90% of ZCash value sits in transparent pools, blockchain surveillance firms can trace the vast majority of ZEC flows with the same fidelity they trace Bitcoin. This is not speculation — Chainalysis lists ZCash as a supported asset in its compliance products marketed to exchanges, banks, and law enforcement agencies.

The shielded pool presents a harder target, but its small size creates a different vulnerability: statistical deanonymization. When only ~10% of transactions use shielding, the anonymity set is small enough that timing analysis, amount correlation, and intersection attacks can narrow down participants. If you shield 47.3 ZEC at 3:14 PM and someone unshields 47.3 ZEC at 3:22 PM, the correlation is trivially observable. In a system where everyone shields, this analysis fails because there are millions of possible matches. In ZCash's small shielded pool, the math works against the user.

Academic research has repeatedly demonstrated these attacks. A 2018 study by researchers at University College London found that the vast majority of ZCash shielded transactions could be linked to transparent addresses using basic heuristics — because users shield from transparent addresses and then unshield back to transparent addresses, creating a "U-turn" pattern that reveals the entire flow.

4.7 — Governance Fracture
The Zcash Foundation vs. ECC Split

ZCash's governance has been defined by a persistent tension between its two funded entities: the Electric Coin Company (ECC), the for-profit company that builds the reference implementation (zcashd), and the Zcash Foundation, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit that was established to provide a counterbalance to ECC's corporate control.

In theory, this dual structure provides checks and balances. In practice, it has produced public disagreements, competing roadmaps, and governance paralysis. The two organizations have clashed over the Dev Fund allocation, protocol priorities, the transition to proof-of-stake, and the fundamental question of whether ZCash should prioritize regulatory compliance or privacy.

The Foundation developed its own node implementation (Zebra, written in Rust) partly to reduce dependence on ECC's zcashd. This created a situation where the two funded entities were building competing infrastructure for the same chain, duplicating effort rather than collaborating. The Foundation pushed for community governance reforms while the ECC resisted changes that might reduce its funding or control.

When Zooko Wilcox stepped down as ECC CEO in 2024, the leadership vacuum deepened the crisis. The new ECC leadership faced declining ZEC price, shrinking Dev Fund revenue (because the tax is denominated in ZEC), and a demoralized community. Proposals for a new funding mechanism for the next halving cycle generated fierce debate, with some community members advocating for ending the Dev Fund entirely.

Single Point of Failure

The fundamental problem is that ZCash depends on funded institutions for its survival. If the ECC runs out of money or the Foundation dissolves, who maintains the protocol? Monero has no such dependency. Its development is distributed across dozens of independent contributors funded ad-hoc through the CCS. No single entity can shut down Monero development because no single entity controls it. ZCash's governance is a corporate org chart. Monero's governance is an open-source bazaar.

4.8 — The Unauditable Supply
The Counterfeiting Problem: Can You Prove Nobody Printed Free Money?

In February 2019, the Zcash team disclosed a critical vulnerability in the original Sprout shielded pool that had existed since launch. The bug, present in the cryptographic circuit used by the Sprout zk-SNARK proofs, could have allowed an attacker to create counterfeit ZEC from nothing — minting coins that never existed, completely invisibly. Because shielded balances are hidden by design, the forged coins would have been indistinguishable from legitimately mined ZEC.

The ECC stated that they found no evidence of exploitation. But this claim contains a logical contradiction: the entire point of the shielded pool is that balances are hidden. If counterfeit coins were created, they would look identical to real coins. There is no mechanism — none — to audit the total supply of ZEC in the shielded pool and verify it matches expected issuance. The ECC's assurance requires trusting them, which is exactly what cryptocurrency was designed to eliminate.

The fix was deployed quietly as part of the Sapling upgrade, before the vulnerability was publicly disclosed. This meant the ZCash network was running with a known-broken cryptographic circuit for approximately two years while the ECC prepared the fix in private. Responsible disclosure is standard practice in security, but the two-year window leaves an uncomfortable question: who else knew?

This vulnerability is not merely historical. It reveals a structural risk inherent to ZCash's design: any bug in the shielded pool's cryptographic proofs could silently inflate the supply, and there is no way for external auditors, node operators, or users to detect it. The shielded pool is a black box by construction.

Monero's Supply Auditability

Monero solves this problem elegantly. ZCash chose hidden amounts with unverifiable supply integrity.

Sound money requires verification, not trust.

Transparent crypto is becoming the regulated norm. Surveillance is expanding at every layer -- exchanges, tax reporting, on-chain analytics, CBDCs. The logical endpoint is not hard to see: organic, structural demand for the one asset that opts out.

73 exchanges delisted Monero in 2025. On-chain activity stayed flat or grew. Tail emission sits at ~0.6 XMR per block -- barely a trickle. Shrinking accessible supply meets growing structural demand. That is not a trade. That is a thesis.
