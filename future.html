Looking Forward

Why Monero Is the Only Future for True Privacy

Legislation is evolving. Surveillance is expanding. Bitcoin is transparent. ZCash is compromised. As the regulatory net tightens, only one cryptocurrency offers persistent, uncompromised financial privacy.

The Regulatory Landscape: 2024-2027

The United States is rapidly building a legal framework for cryptocurrency. These laws grant new freedoms — but they also codify surveillance infrastructure that makes transparent blockchains even more exposed.

May 2024

FIT21 (Financial Innovation and Technology for the 21st Century Act)

Pro-Crypto

Passed House 279-136. Establishes CFTC as primary crypto regulator for decentralized assets. Creates clear classification: digital commodities vs. restricted digital assets vs. payment stablecoins.

Jan 2025

GENIUS Act (Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for U.S. Stablecoins)

Stablecoin

Introduced by Sen. Hagerty. Creates federal licensing framework for stablecoin issuers. Requires 1:1 reserve backing, monthly attestations, and compliance with AML/BSA requirements. Passed Senate Banking Committee March 2025.

Feb 2025

CLARITY Act (Crypto Legal Accessibility, Responsibility, and Innovation for Tokens of Yesterday)

Pro-Crypto

Reintroduced by Rep. McHenry. Exempts certain digital assets from securities classification if sufficiently decentralized. Builds on FIT21's framework. Provides regulatory safe harbor for token developers.

Mar 2025

Executive Order: Strategic Bitcoin Reserve

Government

President Trump signs executive order creating a U.S. Strategic Bitcoin Reserve from seized BTC. Approximately 200,000 BTC held by the government. Signals permanent institutional adoption — and permanent on-chain government surveillance capability.

2025-2026

IRS Digital Asset Reporting Rules (Broker Rules)

Surveillance

IRS finalizes rules requiring centralized exchanges and DeFi front-ends to report all transactions. Cost basis, gains/losses, and wallet addresses tracked. Effective for 2025 tax year. Makes every on-chain Bitcoin transaction cross-referenced with identity.

2025-2027

Travel Rule Expansion (FATF)

Surveillance

Financial Action Task Force expands the Travel Rule to require exchanges to share sender/receiver identity for all crypto transfers above $1,000 (down from $3,000). Applies globally. Makes transparent chain transactions fully identity-linked.

The Freedom Paradox

The CLARITY Act and FIT21 grant Americans genuine freedoms: the right to hold, trade, and build with digital assets without securities classification overreach. These are real and meaningful wins.

But these same laws codify the surveillance infrastructure. When your Bitcoin wallet is linked to your exchange account via IRS broker reporting, and your exchange shares your identity under the Travel Rule, and Chainalysis maps every hop on the transparent ledger — the "freedom" to hold Bitcoin becomes the freedom to hold a fully surveilled asset.

Monero is the only cryptocurrency where these surveillance mechanisms have no effect. There are no amounts to report (RingCT), no addresses to link (stealth addresses), no sender to identify (ring signatures), and no IP to trace (Dandelion++). The IRS bounty for breaking Monero's privacy has been unclaimed since 2020.

The ZCash Fallacy

In 2025, a "privacy meta" emerged on crypto Twitter. Exchanges like Gemini listed ZCash. Industry leaders promoted ZEC as the "compliance-friendly" privacy coin. This narrative is misleading at best, and deliberately deceptive at worst.

A Brief History of ZCash

ZCash (ZEC) launched on October 28, 2016, created by the Electric Coin Company (ECC), founded by Zooko Wilcox-O'Hearn. It was a venture-capital funded project from inception — Pantera Capital, Digital Currency Group, Naval Ravikant, Roger Ver, and Barry Silbert were early investors.

From the start, ZCash was a company-controlled cryptocurrency. The Electric Coin Company owns the trademark, employs the core developers, and directs protocol development. The Zcash Foundation, created later as a "counterbalance," shares governance but remains dependent on the same funding structure.

The Founders' Reward and Dev Tax

For the first four years (2016-2020), 20% of all newly mined ZEC went directly to the founders, investors, and ECC. This "Founders' Reward" distributed approximately 2.1 million ZEC to insiders. After heavy criticism, it was replaced with a "Dev Fund" — which still directs 20% of mining rewards to ECC, the Zcash Foundation, and grants. The community had no meaningful opt-out.

Contrast with Monero: No premine. No founders' reward. No dev tax. No company. No investors. Monero launched with a fair distribution and is funded entirely by community donations through the Community Crowdfunding System (CCS).

The Trusted Setup Problem

ZCash's original zk-SNARKs required a "trusted setup ceremony" — a cryptographic ritual where secret parameters were generated and (supposedly) destroyed. If any participant in the ceremony retained the secret, they could create unlimited counterfeit ZEC undetectably.

While ZCash upgraded to the Sapling ceremony (2018) with 90+ participants and later to Halo 2 (no trusted setup required), the original ceremony's integrity can never be verified retroactively. You are trusting that strangers destroyed cryptographic secrets years ago. Monero requires no such trust — its cryptography is trustless by construction.

The answer is straightforward: ZCash is compliant by design. Its opt-in privacy means exchanges can list it without KYC concerns — 90% of transactions are transparent and auditable. When crypto industry leaders promote ZCash over Monero, they are promoting the appearance of privacy without the reality. ZCash is privacy theater. Monero is the real thing.

Why Monero Threatens the System

Monero is not just a privacy coin. It is a direct challenge to the global financial surveillance infrastructure that underpins modern banking, credit systems, and government monetary control.

The Surveillance Economy

The modern financial system is built on surveillance. Every wire transfer, credit card swipe, and bank deposit is monitored, recorded, and shared with government agencies under the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), FATF recommendations, and FinCEN regulations. This surveillance is not a bug — it is a feature that enables:

  • Monetary policy enforcement — central banks require visibility into money flows to manage inflation, interest rates, and capital controls
  • Tax collection — the IRS cannot tax what it cannot see
  • Sanctions enforcement — OFAC can only blacklist addresses on transparent chains
  • Credit scoring — the entire credit system depends on financial history being observable and verifiable by third parties
  • Petrodollar maintenance — the USD's global reserve status partially depends on the U.S. government's ability to monitor and control dollar flows

Monero makes all of this impossible. Not difficult. Not expensive. Mathematically impossible. There are no amounts to observe, no addresses to blacklist, no flows to trace, and no balances to assess. The IRS has spent $625,000+ trying to break this, and failed.

What 2027+ Looks Like

As the CLARITY Act, GENIUS Act, and Travel Rule expansion take full effect, the gap between transparent and private cryptocurrency will become absolute:

  • Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana — fully integrated into the surveillance-banking complex. Every transaction identity-linked. Every holder KYC'd. Tax-reported automatically. Functionally identical to a bank account with extra steps.
  • ZCash — listed on compliant exchanges precisely because 90% of its transactions are transparent. The 10% shielded usage flagged by regulators. Privacy theater.
  • Monero — the only remaining cryptocurrency where financial privacy is the default, mandatory, and mathematically unbreakable. Delisted from centralized exchanges, thriving in P2P markets, atomic swaps, and Haveno DEX.
