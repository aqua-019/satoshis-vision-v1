Sovereignty vs Surveillance

Monero vs Stablecoins

Stablecoins are the most surveilled financial instruments ever created. They represent the exact opposite of what Satoshi envisioned. Here's why.

The Problem

Stablecoins Are Not Cryptocurrency

They use blockchain technology but violate every principle that made cryptocurrency revolutionary.

The root problem with conventional currency is all the trust that's required to make it work. The central bank must be trusted not to debase the currency.

Satoshi Nakamoto, P2P Foundation, 2009

Stablecoins like USDT and USDC require more trust than traditional banking, not less. You must trust the issuing company, their banking partners, their reserve audits, and the legal jurisdictions they operate in. They can freeze your funds instantly, censor transactions, and share your entire history with governments. They are digital dollars with extra steps.

Blacklist Function

Both USDT and USDC smart contracts contain a blacklist function. The issuing company can add any address to this list, instantly making the funds at that address unmovable. No court order required. No appeal process. Your money is frozen with one transaction.

Counterparty Risk

If Tether or Circle goes bankrupt, faces legal action, or loses banking access, your stablecoins become worthless. You have no claim on reserves and no guaranteed redemption. Monero has no company behind it — it can't be bankrupt, sued, or shut down.

Total Surveillance

Every stablecoin transaction is on a public blockchain with your KYC'd identity attached at the on-ramp. Stablecoin issuers actively work with law enforcement and share transaction data. You have less privacy than a bank account and none of the consumer protections.
